Basic Prep

What to take, what not to take? That is the question, and with good reason. Ensuring you pack and prep properly is important to having a safe and fun experience.

Before you head out for your next outdoor adventure, here's a quick guide to keep in mind to help ensure you have the best experience

Safety

Safety is more than a med kit, it's a mindset. It is preparing appropriately in advance, maintaining spacial awareness when setting camp to avoid potential flash floods or dead trees, staying vigilant when working with fire, sheathing your knife, and maintaining contact with emergency services if at all possible.

Packs

A day pack, as the name suggests is only meant to be large enough for a single days and are typically under 30 liters. Hydration packs are a common favorite since they incorporate your water supply into the bag.

  • Camelbak
  • Geigerrig
  • High Sierra

Backpacking packs are intended to be used over a few days and have a waistbelt to place the weight on the hips rather than the back, relieving back stress and allowing you to go further more comfortably. While usually 40 liters and up, keep in mind that winter conditions require larger sizes (60L+) to accomodate extra clothing layers, warming sleeping bag, and extra food.

  • Osprey
  • Gregory
  • Arcteryx

Knives and Tools

The knife, the quintessential tool for any outdoor goer. But what knife and for how much? 10$, 50$, 100$? If you're the occassional day hiker a cheaper knife should be fine, while a good knife is worth the investment for the more serious adventurer. In either case, a clean, sharp knife is always the safest and while a knife can be used in mutltiple ways, never use the blade to pry something open.

Other useful tools include a can opener, hand shovel, paracord, wetstone, and hand saw.

Provisions

Whether you're going for a day hike or a 7 day excursion through the backcountry, water is always a necessity. For a day trip hydration packs are commonly preferred but for the long haul dromedaries are the way to go.

Snacks and food on the other hand depend on how long your trip is. While a granola bar may suffice for a few miles, dry goods, cheeses, and peanut butter, and other calorie dense foods are the go to for multi-day trips.

Med Kit

Do you really need an entire medkit for a day hike? Depends. Will you have cell signal all the way through to call for medical help in case of an emergency? Are there rattlesnakes or blackwidows in the area? Will you be scrambling over boulders? When you know where you're going and what to expect you can prep your medkit appropriately.

Here's a short list of items we always like to have handy even on the short trips.

  • Purell
  • Alcohol Swabs
  • Antibiotic Cream
  • Sunscreen

Communications

Is your phone enough?

Some important communication tips:

  • Always tell someone where you're going.
  • Charge your comm devices beforehand.
  • Have a way to recharge devices you expect to use often or if on an extended trip.
  • If using radios, agree on which channels to use and under which circumnstances an alternate channel might be needed.
